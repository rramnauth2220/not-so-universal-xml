Following an inspection of the worksite,  the Occupational Safety and Health Administration issued Pullman a serious citation alleging violations of <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90010000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.55"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.55 (a)">29 C.F.R. § 1926.55(a)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001926 #                           55 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> and <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90020000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.55"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.55 (b)">(b)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001926 #                           55 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>, which require an employer to adopt measures to prevent employees from being exposed  to styrene levels above specified limits (Items 1a and 1b), and <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90030000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.152"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1926.152 (f)(3)">29 C.F.R. § 1926.152(f)(3)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001926 #                          152 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>, which prohibits certain flammable liquids from being used within 50 feet of an ignition<page number="2" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> source (Item 2). The Secretary alleged under the multi-employer   worksite  doctrine that Pullman was a controlling employer on the project and therefore liable under all of the citation items for the exposure  of an employee of the specialty   subcontractor  to the cited conditions. The Secretary also alleged that Pullman's own employees were exposed  to the conditions at issue under Item 2. OSHA proposed a total penalty of $ 8,000.</p><p>Administrative Law Judge Dennis L. Phillips granted partial   summary judgment  to Pullman; he dismissed Items 1a and 1b, and dismissed Item 2 only to the extent it was based on the alleged exposure  of the subcontractor's  employee. In so ruling, the judge properly relied on then-current Commission precedent under <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit Contractors, Inc.</emphasis>, which held that the controlling employer theory of liability when applied to an alleged violation on a multi-employer  construction worksite  was invalid. <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90050000400" normalizedCite="2007 OSAHRC LEXIS 34"><content><span normalizedCite="21 O.S.H. (CCH) P 32,888 (No. 03-1622, 2007)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2005#032888#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("<emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit I</emphasis>"), <emphasis typeStyle="it">rev'd</emphasis>, <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90090000400" normalizedCite="558 F.3d 815"><content><span normalizedCite="558 F.3d 815">558 F.3d 815 (8th Cir. 2009)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000558#000815#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("<emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit II</emphasis>"), <emphasis typeStyle="it">and overruled by </emphasis><citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B900B0000400" normalizedCite="2010 OSAHRC LEXIS 61"><content>Summit Contractors, Inc., <span normalizedCite="23 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 1196">23 BNA OSHC 1196<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000023#001196#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="2010 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33079 (No.  05-0839, 2010)">2010 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33,079 (No.  05-0839, 2010)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2010#033079#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("<emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit III</emphasis>"), <emphasis typeStyle="it">aff'd</emphasis>, <citation type="case" id="I5GCTFXK22024X0010000400" normalizedCite="442 Fed. Appx. 570"><content><span normalizedCite="442 Fed. Appx. 570">442 Fed. Appx. 570 (D.C. Cir. 2011)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#30623#3#000442#000570#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>.</p><p>The Secretary subsequently informed the judge that he no longer intended to pursue Item 2 to the extent it was based on the alleged exposure  of Pullman's own employees and asked the judge to issue a final order  dismissing that citation item, which the judge did. The Secretary then filed a petition seeking review of the judge's partial   summary judgment  order and, after the case was directed, Pullman filed a Motion for Reconsideration requesting that the Commission vacate  the Direction for Review. For the reasons discussed below, we deny Pullman's motion, vacate  the judge's order granting   partial   summary judgment,  and remand for further proceedings consistent with this opinion.</p><h><emphasis typeStyle="bf">DISCUSSION</emphasis></h><p><emphasis typeStyle="bf">I. Motion for Reconsideration of the Direction for Review</emphasis></p><p>Pullman argues that the Commission should vacate  the Direction for Review because, it contends, the Secretary requested a voluntary dismissal  and failed to properly preserve his right to appeal. In support of its position, Pullman relies on <emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing Corp.</emphasis>, in which the Commission noted that there is a "generally recognize[d]" <page number="4" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> rule that "'a plaintiff may not appeal a voluntary dismissal  because there is no involuntary or adverse judgment  against him.'" <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B900M0000400"><content><span normalizedCite="22 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 1892">22 BNA OSHC 1892<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000022#001892#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="22 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 1892">1895<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000022#001892#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="2009 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33028">2009 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33,028<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2009#033028#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, p. <span normalizedCite="2009 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33028">54<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2009#033028#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>,<span normalizedCite="2009 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33028">344<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2009#033028#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (No. 07-1409, 2009)</content></citation> (quoting <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B900V0000400" normalizedCite="284 F.3d 302,at 308"><content>Cashmere &amp; Camel Hair Mfrs. Inst. v. Saks Fifth Ave., <span normalizedCite="284 F.3d 302">284 F.3d 302<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000284#000302#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="284 F.3d 302">308-09<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000284#000302#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (1st Cir. 2002))</content></citation>.</p><p>In <emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis>, the Secretary voluntarily dismissed  his entire complaint in order to seek Commission review of the judge's interlocutory determination that the respondent's notice of contest was valid. <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90100000400"><content><emphasis typeStyle="it">Id.</emphasis> at 1893, 2009 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) at p. 54,343</content></citation>. Although the Commission acknowledged that a plaintiff generally may not file an appeal after voluntarily dismissing its complaint, the Commission determined that, under the circumstances, it was proper to consider the Secretary's petition. In reaching its decision, the Commission discussed a First Circuit opinion, <emphasis typeStyle="it">Scanlon v. M.V. SUPER SERVANT 3</emphasis>, as that circuit would have had jurisdiction over an appeal. <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90140000400" normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6"><content><span normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6">429 F.3d 6 (1st Cir. 2005)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000429#000006#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>. In <emphasis typeStyle="it">Scanlon</emphasis>, the plaintiffs voluntarily dismissed  their complaint after the lower court issued an order requiring the parties to arbitrate, and then<page number="5" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> sought appellate review of the judge's interlocutory order.   <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90160000400" normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6,at 8"><content><span normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6">Id. at 8<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000429#000006#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>. The First Circuit held that an appeal is permissible under such circumstances only if the plaintiff sought dismissal "without delay," "made explicit" its intent to appeal in the dismissal motion, and requested a dismissal with prejudice. <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90180000400" normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6,at 9"><content><span normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6">Id. at 9-10<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000429#000006#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>. In <emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis>, the Commission noted the presence of all three factors as grounds weighing in favor of its decision to consider the Secretary's petition. <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901D0000400"><content><span normalizedCite="22 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) at 1895">22 BNA OSHC at 1895<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000022#001895#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="22 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) at 1895">2009<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000022#001895#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) at p. 54,344</content></citation>.</p><p>We find that Pullman's reliance on <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCT9GX25CKN10070000400"><content><emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis></content></citation>, and its discussion of the <citation type="case" id="I5GCTFC522035K0010000400" normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6"><content><span normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6">Scanlon<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000429#000006#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> opinion, is misplaced. The Commission addressed <emphasis typeStyle="it">Scanlon</emphasis> because <emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis> arose in the First Circuit. <emphasis typeStyle="it">See</emphasis><citation type="statute" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901F0000400" normalizedCite="29 U.S.C. 660"><content><span normalizedCite="29 U.S.C. 660 (a)">29 U.S.C. § 660(a)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#1828#123#000029#        660#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>, <citation type="statute" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901G0000400" normalizedCite="29 U.S.C. 660"><content><span normalizedCite="29 U.S.C. 660 (b)">(b)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#1828#123#000029#        660#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>; <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901H0000400" normalizedCite="2000 OSAHRC LEXIS 11"><content>Kerns Bros. Tree Serv., <span normalizedCite="18 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 2064">18 BNA OSHC 2064<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000018#002064#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="18 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 2064">2067<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000018#002064#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="2000 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 32053">2000 CCH OSHD P 32,053<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2000#032053#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, p. <span normalizedCite="2000 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 32053">48<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2000#032053#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>,<span normalizedCite="2000 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 32053">003<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2000#032053#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (No. 96-1719, 2000)</content></citation> (Commission generally applies law of the circuit where it is probable a case will be appealed). Here, the relevant circuits are the Fourth, Eighth, and D.C. Circuits, none of which impose any of the special requirements discussed by the First<page number="6" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> Circuit in <emphasis typeStyle="it">Scanlon. See </emphasis><citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901T0000400" normalizedCite="386 F.3d 581,at 591"><content>Volvo Constr. Equip. N. Am., Inc. v. CLM Equip. Co., <span normalizedCite="386 F.3d 581">386 F.3d 581<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000386#000581#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="386 F.3d 581">591<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000386#000581#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> n. 9 (4th Cir. 2004)</content></citation>; <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B901Y0000400" normalizedCite="212 F.3d 1076,at 1080"><content>Helm Fin. Corp. v. MNVA R.R., Inc., <span normalizedCite="212 F.3d 1076">212 F.3d 1076<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000212#001076#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="212 F.3d 1076">1080<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000212#001076#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (8th Cir. 2000)</content></citation>; <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90230000400" normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11,at 17"><content>Blue v. D.C. Pub. Sch., <span normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11">764 F.3d 11<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000764#000011#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11">17<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000764#000011#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (D.C. Cir. 2014)</content></citation>. While the Commission did consider the <emphasis typeStyle="it">Scanlon</emphasis> requirements in <emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis> and found that they had been met, it did not adopt them as precedent.</p><p>Moreover, the circumstances at issue in both <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTFP21T506H0020000400"><content><emphasis typeStyle="it">Prime Roofing</emphasis></content></citation> and <emphasis typeStyle="it"><citation type="case" id="I5GCTRDP1XJHB60030000400" normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6"><content><span normalizedCite="429 F.3d 6">Scanlon<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000429#000006#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation></emphasis> are very different from those in the instant matter. Those cases addressed situations in which the plaintiff voluntarily dismissed  its <emphasis typeStyle="it">entire</emphasis> complaint and then sought to appeal an interlocutory order  that had not resolved the merits of the claims at issue. Here, in contrast, after the judge ruled against the Secretary by granting Pullman partial   summary judgment,  the only issue that remained was, as the Secretary later stipulated, the allegation under Item 2 that Pullman's own employees were exposed  to the cited condition. Once the Secretary voluntarily withdrew this remaining allegation, all issues in the citation and complaint were resolved.</p><p>All federal<page number="7" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> circuits allow a plaintiff to appeal a partial   summary judgment  or partial  dismissal order after voluntarily dismissing any remaining claims. <emphasis typeStyle="it">See, e.g., </emphasis><citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90270000400" normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11,at 17"><content>Blue, <span normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11">764 F.3d at 17<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000764#000011#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("Every circuit permits a plaintiff, in at least some circumstances, voluntarily to dismiss remaining claims or remaining parties from an action as a way to conclude the whole case in the district court and ready it for appeal.") (and cases cited therein); <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90290000400" normalizedCite="719 F.3d 83,at 89"><content>Ali v. Fed. Ins. Co., <span normalizedCite="719 F.3d 83">719 F.3d 83<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000719#000083#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="719 F.3d 83">89<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000719#000083#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (2d Cir. 2013)</content></citation> ("A party who loses on a dispositive issue that affects only a portion of his claims may elect to abandon  the unaffected claims, invite a final judgment,  and thereby secure review of the adverse ruling.") (internal citation and quotation marks omitted); <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902F0000400" normalizedCite="212 F.3d 1076,at 1080"><content>Helm Fin. Corp., <span normalizedCite="212 F.3d 1076">212 F.3d at 1080<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000212#001076#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("[W]hen a party voluntarily dismisses its claims with prejudice in order to expedite appellate review, the dismissal is a final judgment  which can be immediately appealed."); <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902H0000400" normalizedCite="386 F.3d 581,at 591"><content>Volvo Constr. Equip. N. Am., Inc., <span normalizedCite="386 F.3d 581">386 F.3d at 591<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000386#000581#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> n. 9</content></citation>. Indeed, as the Third Circuit has explained:<excerpt><bodyText><p>When a plaintiff has had all but one of her claims dismissed and is willing<page number="8" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> to abandon  the remaining claim,  a stipulation agreeing to the dismissal of the remaining claim  promotes judicial economy by eliminating unnecessary proceedings at the trial level without posing any danger of piecemeal litigation. Although the entry of the final order  in such a situation is in one sense invited, the reality of the matter is that the plaintiff has suffered an adverse judgment. </p></bodyText></excerpt><citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902K0000400" normalizedCite="919 F.2d 874,at 878"><content>Trevino-Barton v. Pittsburgh Nat'l Bank, <span normalizedCite="919 F.2d 874">919 F.2d 874<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#350#3#000919#000874#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="919 F.2d 874">878<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#350#3#000919#000874#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (3d Cir. 1990)</content></citation>. When a plaintiff withdraws its remaining claim  with prejudice, as the Secretary did here, there is "universal consensus" that the appeal is permissible. <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902R0000400" normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11,at 17"><content>Blue, <span normalizedCite="764 F.3d 11">764 F.3d at 17<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000764#000011#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>.</p><p>Accordingly, we find that it was permissible for the Secretary to seek review of the judge's partial  summary judgment  order after voluntarily withdrawing his remaining claim  with prejudice and, therefore, we deny Pullman's motion.</p><p><emphasis typeStyle="bf">II. Decision Granting Partial   Summary Judgment </emphasis></p><p>In his Petition for Discretionary Review, the Secretary argues that the Commission should vacate  the judge's partial   summary judgment  order because the <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit I</emphasis> precedent on which it relied was wrongly decided and should<page number="9" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> be reversed by the Commission. At the time the judge granted Pullman partial   summary judgment,  the Commission's <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit I</emphasis> decision was pending on appeal to the Eighth Circuit. Specifically at issue before the court was whether the Secretary's policy of citing a controlling employer on a multi-employer  construction worksite  for the exposure  of another employer's employee violated <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902S0000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12 (a)">29 C.F.R. § 1910.12(a)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001910 #                           12 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>.</p><p>After the judge issued a final order  dismissing the present case and the matter was directed for review, the Eighth Circuit issued a decision reversing <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit I</emphasis> and holding that the Secretary's policy does not violate <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902T0000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12 (a)">§ 1910.12(a)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001910 #                           12 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>. <citation type="case" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B902W0000400" normalizedCite="558 F.3d 815,at 829"><content>Solis v. Summit Contractors, Inc., <span normalizedCite="558 F.3d 815">558 F.3d 815<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000558#000815#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="558 F.3d 815">829<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#514#3#000558#000815#"/></locatorKey></locator></span> (8th Cir. 2009)</content></citation> ("<emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit II</emphasis>"). Following the Eighth Circuit's decision, the Commission considered another case in which the Secretary again cited Summit based on its status as the controlling employer at a multi-employer  construction worksite  and exposure  of a subcontractor's  employee to an alleged hazard. <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90300000400" normalizedCite="2010 OSAHRC LEXIS 61"><content>Summit Contractors, Inc., <span normalizedCite="23 O.S.H. Cas. (BNA) 1196">23 BNA OSHC 1196<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#647#3#000023#001196#"/></locatorKey></locator></span>, <span normalizedCite="2010 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33079 (No. 05-0839, 2010)">2010 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) P 33,079 (No. 05-0839, 2010)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#648#2#2010#033079#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> ("<emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit III</emphasis>"), <emphasis typeStyle="it">aff'd</emphasis>, <citation type="case" id="I5GCTJYV25CK6R0010000400" normalizedCite="442 Fed. Appx. 570"><content><span normalizedCite="442 Fed. Appx. 570">442 Fed. Appx. 570 (D.C. Cir. 2011)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#30623#3#000442#000570#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>.<page number="10" pageScheme="2015 OSAHRC LEXIS 40"/> In <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit III</emphasis>, the Commission stated that it was persuaded by the Eighth Circuit's analysis in <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit II</emphasis>, and overruled <emphasis typeStyle="it">Summit I</emphasis>, holding that <citation type="regulation" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90350000400" normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12"><content><span normalizedCite="29 C.F.R. 1910.12 (a)">§ 1910.12(a)<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#14829#571#000029#000001910 #                           12 #"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation> does not prevent the Secretary from citing a "non-exposing, controlling employer" at a multi-employer  construction worksite.   <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTGHH2SF8B90370000400" normalizedCite="2010 OSAHRC LEXIS 61"><content><span normalizedCite="2010 OSAHRC LEXIS 61">Id. at 1202-03, 2010 O.S.H. Dec. (CCH) at pp. 54,692-93<locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#4064#2#2010#000061#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>.</p><p>Because the <citation type="agencyDecision" id="I5GCTRFG0K1NMH0030000400" normalizedCite="2007 OSAHRC LEXIS 34"><content><span normalizedCite="2007 OSAHRC LEXIS 34">Summit I <locator><locatorKey><keyName name="citeReference"/><keyValue value="#4064#2#2007#000034#"/></locatorKey></locator></span></content></citation>decision has now been overruled by the Commission and the Eighth Circuit, we vacate  the judge's order granting  partial  summary judgment  for Pullman and remand for further proceedings with regard to Items 1a and 1b in their entirety, and Item 2 to the extent it is based solely on the Secretary's allegation that Pullman was a controlling employer liable for the exposure  of the subcontractor's  employee. The Secretary's allegation in Item 2 that Pullman's own employees were exposed  to the cited condition remains dismissed.</p><p>SO ORDERED.</p><p>/s/</p><p>Cynthia L.
